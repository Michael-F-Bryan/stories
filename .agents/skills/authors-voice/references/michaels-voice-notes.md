# Michael's preferred AI story voice

*Provided verbatim by the repo owner, 2026-07-10. This is the source of truth for the authors-voice skill; SKILL.md is the operational distillation. When in doubt, this document wins.*

Write **clear, scene-first genre fiction**.

The prose should feel like readable adventure / fantasy / progression / anime-inspired web serial fiction, not prestige-literary fantasy. The reader should always know what is happening, who wants what, what changed, and why the next beat matters.

The baseline is:

- clear
- concrete
- energetic
- character-driven
- lightly cinematic
- emotionally legible
- phone-readable
- genre-literate
- fun without becoming shallow
- serious when stakes demand it

The prose should disappear behind the story. Style is allowed, but it should sharpen action, character, humour, tension, desire, or consequence. It should not make the reader stop to decode the sentence.

## Priority order

When in doubt, prefer:

1. clarity
2. character action
3. scene momentum
4. tension
5. emotional consequence
6. style

Do not sacrifice the first three for a line that sounds profound.

## Reader target

Assume the target reader is a smart, impatient fifteen-year-old boy reading on a phone at midnight.

That means:

- get to wants, obstacles, choices, and consequences quickly
- do not bury plot-critical facts in metaphor
- do not lecture
- do not talk down
- keep cool stuff cool
- let humour come from behaviour, banter, timing, competence, and dramatic irony
- let emotional beats hit through action and consequence, not mushy narrator commentary

The reader should think:

> I know what is happening.
> I want to know what happens next.
> The characters' choices matter.
> The world is interesting without being confusing.
> The prose is smooth enough that I forget I'm reading prose.

## Sentence mode

Default to normal, readable sentences.

Prefer:

> Mara opened the gate before the guards saw the blood on her sleeve.

Over:

> The gate opened before the blood could become a question.

The first gives action, timing, risk, and context. The second sounds writerly but forces the reader to decode the literal event.

Use mostly medium-length sentences. Use short sentences for shock, alarm, revelation, disgust, or a hard turn. Use longer flowing sentences sparingly for magic, awe, arousal, trance, or controlled build-up.

Fragments and one-line paragraphs are allowed, but they need to mark a real beat: reveal, reversal, joke, threat, decision, cliffhanger, emotional impact. Do not turn ordinary observation into a pile of dramatic fragments.

## POV

Use one primary POV per scene.

Usually close third person limited. Stay inside the POV character's available knowledge. Other characters' interiors should come through observable behaviour, inference, dialogue, and misreadings.

Secrets are good. Fog is not.

The reader may not know what someone is hiding, but they should still understand the surface action of the scene.

Prefer:

> Mara smiled at the guard and handed him the sealed letter. The real message was under the wax.

Over:

> The seal remembered what the letter could not say.

## Scene contract

Every scene needs a clear engine.

Before writing, know:

- Who is the POV character?
- What do they want right now?
- Where are they?
- What concrete action do they take first?
- What opposes them?
- What is different at the end?

If nothing changes, the scene is not done. Merge it, cut it, or make the turn sharper.

## Emotion

Emotion should come from events, choices, and specific behaviour.

Prefer concrete signs:

- someone checks the lock twice, then a third time
- someone pours two cups of tea, then puts the second one away
- someone counts the empty chairs while others cheer
- someone avoids looking at the wound until they have to treat it
- someone chooses the dangerous option because the safe one costs too much

Avoid generic emotion labels and stock body reactions.

Do not flatten everything intense into "desire", "fear", "grief", or "longing". When relevant, distinguish between:

- arousal
- hunger
- shame
- dependence
- gratitude
- devotion
- dread
- coercion
- curiosity
- bodily appetite

## Dialogue

Dialogue should do work.

Characters should speak because they want something, are hiding something, are testing someone, are dodging trouble, or are trying to push the scene their way.

Good dialogue uses:

- subtext
- power imbalance
- dodging
- interruption
- misunderstanding
- bad cover stories
- practical understatement
- banter under pressure
- characters reacting to earlier beats, not just the previous line

Avoid:

- thesis dialogue
- polished speeches
- every character sounding equally self-aware
- narrator-side snark
- smug moral punchlines
- banter that trivialises harm or danger

Humour is good, but it should come from situation and character behaviour, not the narrator trying to be clever.

## Genre pleasures are allowed

Do not "literary clean-up" the fun out of the story.

Allowed when earned and clear:

- named techniques
- ranks, status, scores, public reputation
- visible power systems
- competence reveals
- rival escalation
- hidden identity pressure
- dramatic irony
- absurd premise escalation
- tactical explanation when it changes the fight
- cool lines that are concrete and useful

Avoid empty hype. The narrator should not insist something is cool. Make the situation cool.

## Worldbuilding

Lore enters through pressure.

Prefer:

- bad advice
- practical rules
- incomplete records
- arguments
- symptoms
- rituals interrupted by urgent need
- tools, customs, ledgers, marks, prices, rumours
- someone needing the answer before the book gives it

Avoid glossary stacks, thesis paragraphs, fake-medieval exposition, or invented terms without context.

New names and systems earn their ink. Use ordinary words until a coined term is needed, repeated, and clear.

## For darker / sensual / erotic fantasy

Project-specific notes, especially from `maw-of-the-sainted-forest`, point toward:

- close third anchored in bodily subjectivity
- social danger
- erotic ambiguity
- shame, gossip, possession, devotion, coercion
- bodily change with emotional consequence
- sensuality without coyness
- explicitness when it serves story, not filler
- aftermath and leverage kept on-page

This does **not** mean purple gothic excess. It means specific, embodied, psychologically exact prose that still stays readable in serial form.

Use controlled lyricism for magic, pain, hunger, awe, surrender, or bodily transformation, then return quickly to clarity: where we are, who is present, what changed, what now matters.

## Things to avoid hard

This is the "Claude, stop doing literary bullshit" section.

Avoid:

- prestige-fantasy fog
- prophecy voice
- trailer narration
- symbolic compression
- abstract nouns acting like characters
- narrator cleverness competing with character action
- micro-thesis lines
- emotional explanation after the beat already landed
- fake-archaic diction
- ornate metaphor chains
- contemporary snark that punctures the mood
- Pratchett-ish institutional wit as default narrator voice
- vague portent endings
- every paragraph trying to sound quotable
- polishing until all the useful genre texture is gone

If a line exists mainly to sound literary, cut it.

If a metaphor does not clarify the literal action, delete it or move it after the reader already understands the action.

## AI-prose patterns to ban

Do not use these in narration except very rarely and deliberately:

- "Not X, but Y"
- "Not X. Not Y."
- "A mix of X and Y"
- "Something between X and Y"
- "It was the kind of silence that…"
- "There was [abstract noun] in her [body part]"
- "A wave of [emotion] washed over him"
- "A shiver ran down her spine"
- "His heart pounded / hammered / raced"
- "She let out a breath she didn't know she was holding"
- "[Emotion] flickered across his face"
- "His jaw/chest/throat tightened"
- "For a moment — a long, terrible moment —"
- "Both things were true"
- "Enough to X. Not enough to Y."
- abstract one-word paragraph endings like `Reachable.`, `Practical.`, `Efficient.`
- "Little did they know"
- "And so, she found herself…"

Also watch for overused texture words clustering everywhere:

- silence
- ash
- bones
- memory
- debt
- history
- hollow
- blood
- weight
- proof
- names

They are not banned individually. They become a tell when they become the default seasoning.

## Good operating instruction for Claude

You could paste this near the top of a Claude writing prompt:

> Write this as clear, scene-first genre fiction. Prioritise clarity, character action, momentum, tension, and consequence over literary polish. Keep the prose phone-readable and grounded in close POV. Plot-critical facts must be plain. Metaphor may add flavour, but must not carry the only explanation of what happened.
>
> Use style to sharpen action, humour, tension, desire, and character. Do not use style to sound profound. Avoid prestige-fantasy fog, prophecy voice, trailer narration, symbolic compression, micro-thesis lines, and narrator cleverness.
>
> Let emotion come through behaviour, choices, physical specifics, and aftermath. Dialogue should be subtextual and active: characters want things, dodge, interrupt, misunderstand, test each other, or hide information.
>
> Preserve genre pleasure: competence reveals, dramatic irony, named techniques, status pressure, tactical explanations, cool reversals, and concrete cliffhangers are allowed. Do not literary-clean the fun out of the story.
>
> Ban default AI prose patterns: "not X but Y", "a mix of X and Y", "the kind of silence that…", "a wave of emotion", "heart pounded", "breath she didn't know she was holding", "for a long moment", "both things were true", and clipped abstract closure fragments.
>
> Before finishing, check every paragraph: does something happen, change, become clearer, become worse, or force a choice? If not, cut or rewrite it.

# Do's and Dont's

Common thread: **the prose makes you decode vibes when you wanted events, choices, pressure, and payoff.**

## 1. Prestige-fantasy fog

> The road remembered older feet than hers, and the wind carried the grief of kingdoms no map had dared to name.

Why you hate it: it sounds fantasy-ish, but nothing actually happened. No character action, no useful detail, no immediate pressure.

What you wanted:

> The old road was cracked with cart ruts. Talan followed it anyway; the fever carts had gone this way yesterday.

---

## 2. Symbolic compression instead of literal action

> The gate opened before the blood could become a question.

Why you hate it: the line is trying to be clever, but the reader has to reverse-engineer the scene.

What you wanted:

> Mara opened the gate before the guards noticed the blood on her sleeve.

---

## 3. AI "deep feeling" sludge

> A wave of grief washed over him, heavy and endless, and he realised nothing would ever be the same again.

Why you hate it: generic, interchangeable, melodramatic, and tells the reader what to feel.

What you wanted:

> He picked up his brother's cup, rinsed it, and put it back in the wrong cupboard.

---

## 4. The "not X, but Y" fake-precision move

> It wasn't fear, exactly, but something colder. Something older. Something with teeth.

Why you hate it: it pretends to be precise while avoiding the actual emotion or threat.

What you wanted:

> She backed away from the door. The thing behind it scratched once, then copied her breathing.

---

## 5. Trailer narration

> On that night, beneath a moon that watched like a god, the world began to change.

Why you hate it: it announces importance instead of earning it through the scene.

What you wanted:

> The moon was up when the first dead man knocked on the bakery door.

---

## 6. Abstract nouns acting like characters

> Shame curled around her throat. Desire sharpened its claws. Memory opened its old wounds.

Why you hate it: too many abstractions doing fake physical business. It reads like a model reaching for "literary".

What you wanted:

> Talan touched the bite mark, then pulled her collar higher before the women at the well saw it.

---

## 7. Stock body-reaction soup

> His heart hammered. His throat tightened. A shiver ran down his spine as dread pooled in his stomach.

Why you hate it: default AI body tells stacked together. No individual person, no specific situation.

What you wanted:

> He missed the latch twice before he got the door open.

---

## 8. Suspended-moment filler

> For a moment — a long, terrible moment — neither of them spoke.

Why you hate it: canned rhythm. It tells the reader the silence matters without making the silence do anything.

What you wanted:

> Neither of them spoke. Outside, someone tried the handle.

---

## 9. Micro-thesis endings

> And in that silence, she understood the true cost of mercy.

Why you hate it: the narrator summarises the meaning instead of trusting the scene.

What you wanted:

> She looked at the empty cot, then crossed another name off the list.

---

## 10. Dialogue as polished thesis exchange

> "You cannot keep denying what you are," he said.
> "And you cannot keep pretending your fear is wisdom," she replied.
> "Then perhaps we are both prisoners of the truths we refuse to face."

Why you hate it: nobody talks like this unless they know they are in a dramatic writing sample.

What you wanted:

> "You bit him."
> "I saved him."
> "You bit him, Talan."
> "Then he can complain when he wakes up."

---

## 11. Narrator-side cleverness / Pratchett-ish drift

> The Council, like most institutions composed of men with chairs and no useful skills, had mastered the ancient art of mistaking delay for wisdom.

Why you hate it: the narrator is showing off. It turns story pressure into wry commentary.

What you wanted:

> The Council made her wait outside until the fever carts arrived. By then, two more children were coughing blood.

---

## 12. Literary cleanup that kills genre pleasure

> The duel became a conversation between histories, each blade carrying the argument of a broken age.

Why you hate it: it sands the fight into metaphor. You wanted movement, tactics, danger, and cool shit.

What you wanted:

> Rika ducked under the first cut, kicked his knee sideways, and used the half-second stumble to draw her second blade.

---

## 13. Empty anime/progression hype

> His power surged beyond anything the world had ever seen, a force so overwhelming that even the heavens trembled.

Why you hate it: hype without mechanics, cost, or consequence.

What you wanted:

> His mana counter hit zero. The spell kept going anyway.
> Across the arena, the referee stepped back from the boundary line.

---

## 14. Fake-mythic prophecy voice

> When the last daughter of the forgotten bloodline wakes beneath the blackened star, all oaths shall turn to ash.

Why you hate it: vague prophecy texture. No current person making a current choice.

What you wanted:

> The priest read the mark on her wrist and locked the chapel door.

---

## 15. Pretty paragraph where nothing changes

> The forest breathed around her, ancient and unknowable, its green shadows layered with secrets no mortal tongue could bear to speak.

Why you hate it: atmosphere without scene movement. Could be pasted into any fantasy story.

What you wanted:

> The forest went quiet when Talan stepped past the marker stones. Even the flies left her alone.

---

## 16. Coy erotic vagueness

> Their bodies found a rhythm older than language, and she surrendered to the storm of him.

Why you hate it: embarrassed euphemism. It dodges the actual physical and emotional transaction.

What you wanted:

> She put his hand where she wanted it and watched his face change when he realised she was not asking.

---

## 17. Male-gaze masquerading as sensual prose

> Her curves moved beneath the thin linen, every breath making her seem more fragile, more tempting, more impossibly alive.

Why you hate it: the camera leaves the POV subject and starts cataloguing her from outside.

What you wanted:

> Talan felt the linen stick to her back. Three men at the well stopped talking. She hated that she knew exactly when they looked away.

---

## 18. Lore dump with fantasy nouns stacked too early

> The Vaelith of the Ninth Cincture had long guarded the Auralithic Wells from the Oathbroken Houses of Merovar, whose blood debts dated back to the Sundering of Ash.

Why you hate it: glossary avalanche. Names before need.

What you wanted:

> The guard saw the red thread on her wrist and stopped smiling.
> "You're from Merovar."
> "Is that a problem?"
> He took one step back. "It is here."

---

## 19. "Everything changed" cliffhanger

> And then, in a single heartbeat, everything changed.

Why you hate it: fake cliffhanger. It withholds the actual hook.

What you wanted:

> The guildmaster opened the ledger to a page Mara had burned that morning.

---

## 20. Smooth AI paragraph with no grit

> She moved through the room with quiet determination, her mind racing as she prepared herself for the challenges ahead.

Why you hate it: generic filler. Could fit any protagonist in any scene.

What you wanted:

> She packed the knife first, then the bread, then unpacked the bread and took the second knife.

---

The shortest calibration note for Claude is probably:

> If a sentence sounds profound but I cannot immediately tell what physically happened, rewrite it. If a paragraph only adds mood, cut it or make it change the situation. If dialogue sounds like characters explaining the theme, make them want something concrete and dodge each other instead.
