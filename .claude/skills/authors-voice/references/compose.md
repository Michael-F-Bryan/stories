# Positive composition guidance

## Character-owned attention

Build prose from what the POV character notices, needs, misreads, and can act on. Attention should follow pressure, practical use, and current uncertainty.

Use the story-engine generation packet as orientation for reader pull, promise movement, character pressure, attention field, causal spine, shape choice, explanation budget, and voice anchors. Its one short ordinary spoken sample and one short ordinary internal sample orient tone, syntax, attention, and pressure only; they are not copyable templates or catchphrases.

## Social action beneath dialogue

Each speaker is doing something with the exchange: requesting, dodging, testing, concealing, recruiting, stalling, soothing, dominating, bargaining, or deflecting. Dialogue can be messy, partial, interrupted, indirect, and unequal, because people use speech to move the room.

## Uneven narrative weight

Spend more narrative attention on pressure, change, and consequence. Give routine movement, obvious transitions, and plain connective steps enough words to stay clear, but less emphasis. Let memorable lines arise from the scene rather than from constant optimization.

## Ordinary connective tissue

Plain transitions, workmanlike verbs, incomplete thoughts, asymmetry, and unpolished bridge sentences are useful. They keep literal action clear and leave room for the strong beats to matter.

## Clear causality and grounded terms

Keep spatial causality easy to follow. Introduce world terms through need, use, and context so the reader learns them while the scene is moving.

## Genre pleasure

Keep wonder sincere, humour rooted in behaviour, competence visible, training and status pressure readable, tactical mechanics meaningful, and emotional turns earned. Let the scene feel alive without the prose announcing its own cleverness.

## Rhythm

Let rhythm follow thought and action. Vary sentence length naturally. Use the scene's pressure to decide when a line should be plain and when it should lean.
