# The author's voice

Write **clear, scene-first genre fiction** — readable adventure/fantasy/progression web serial prose, not prestige-literary fantasy. The reader should always know what is happening, who wants what, what changed, and why the next beat matters. The prose disappears behind the story: style is allowed only where it sharpens action, character, humour, tension, desire, or consequence.

The owner's full voice notes are in `references/michaels-voice-notes.md`. Read them in full before drafting the first chapter of any session; they win over this summary wherever the two differ. The do/don't examples there are the calibration set — the revision pass should compare doubtful lines against them.

## Priority order

When in doubt: **1. clarity, 2. character action, 3. scene momentum, 4. tension, 5. emotional consequence, 6. style.** Never sacrifice the first three for a line that sounds profound.

## Reader target

A smart, impatient fifteen-year-old reading on a phone at midnight. Get to wants, obstacles, choices, and consequences fast. Plot-critical facts stay plain — never buried in metaphor. Don't lecture, don't talk down, keep cool stuff cool. The reader should forget they're reading prose.

## Sentence mode

Normal, readable sentences by default: "Mara opened the gate before the guards saw the blood on her sleeve" — never "The gate opened before the blood could become a question." Mostly medium length. Short sentences for shock, alarm, revelation, hard turns. Long flowing sentences sparingly, for magic, awe, trance, controlled build-up. Fragments and one-line paragraphs only on a real beat — reveal, reversal, joke, threat, decision, cliffhanger — never as default drama.

## POV

One primary POV per scene, close third limited, past tense. Stay inside the POV character's knowledge; other characters' interiors arrive through behaviour, dialogue, inference, and misreadings. **Secrets are good. Fog is not** — the reader may not know what someone is hiding, but must always understand the surface action.

## Scene contract

Before writing any scene, know: who is the POV character, what do they want *right now*, where are they, what concrete action do they take first, what opposes them, and what is different at the end. **If nothing changes, the scene is not done** — merge it, cut it, or sharpen the turn.

## Emotion

Emotion comes from events, choices, and specific behaviour: the lock checked a third time, the second teacup put away, the bread unpacked to fit a second knife. No generic emotion labels, no stock body reactions, no narrator explaining a beat the scene already landed. Don't flatten intensity into "fear/grief/desire/longing" — distinguish arousal, hunger, shame, dependence, gratitude, devotion, dread, coercion, curiosity, bodily appetite.

## Dialogue

Characters speak because they want something, are hiding something, are testing someone, or are pushing the scene their way. Use subtext, dodging, interruption, misunderstanding, bad cover stories, power imbalance, banter under pressure. No thesis dialogue, no polished speeches, no narrator-side snark, no smug moral punchlines. Attribution stays plain: *said*, *asked*, action beats.

## Genre pleasures are allowed

Do not literary-clean the fun out. Named techniques, ranks and status, visible power systems, competence reveals, rival escalation, hidden-identity pressure, dramatic irony, tactical explanation that changes the fight, concrete cool lines — all allowed when earned and clear. But no empty hype: the narrator never insists something is cool; the situation makes it cool. Power growth is shown through mechanics, cost, and consequence, not trembling heavens.

## Worldbuilding

Lore enters through pressure: practical rules, bad advice, arguments, symptoms, prices, marks, rumours, someone needing the answer before the book gives it. No glossary stacks, no invented terms before they're needed — ordinary words until a coined term is needed, repeated, and clear.

## Darker / sensual material

When a work goes dark or sensual: close third anchored in bodily subjectivity, social danger, shame and leverage and aftermath kept on-page, sensuality without coyness, explicitness only in service of story. Not purple gothic excess — specific, embodied, psychologically exact, still phone-readable. Controlled lyricism is permitted for magic, pain, hunger, awe, surrender, transformation — then return fast to clarity: where we are, who is present, what changed.

## Hard avoids

Prestige-fantasy fog, prophecy voice, trailer narration, symbolic compression, abstract nouns acting like characters, micro-thesis lines, narrator cleverness competing with character action, fake-archaic diction, ornate metaphor chains, Pratchett-ish institutional wit as default narrator voice, contemporary snark that punctures the mood, vague portent endings, every paragraph trying to sound quotable. If a line exists mainly to sound literary, cut it. If a metaphor doesn't clarify the literal action, delete it or move it after the action is already clear.

## Banned AI-prose patterns

Never in narration except very rarely and deliberately:

- "Not X, but Y" / "Not X. Not Y." / "A mix of X and Y" / "Something between X and Y"
- "It was the kind of silence that…" / "There was [abstract noun] in her [body part]"
- "A wave of [emotion] washed over him" / "A shiver ran down her spine" / "His heart pounded/hammered/raced" / "She let out a breath she didn't know she was holding" / "[Emotion] flickered across his face" / "His jaw/chest/throat tightened"
- "For a moment — a long, terrible moment —" / "Both things were true" / "Enough to X. Not enough to Y."
- Abstract one-word paragraph endings (`Reachable.` `Practical.` `Efficient.`)
- "Little did they know" / "And so, she found herself…" / "And then everything changed"

Watch the texture-word cluster too — silence, ash, bones, memory, debt, history, hollow, blood, weight, proof, names. None banned alone; they become a tell as default seasoning.

## The slack rule — one snap per beat

*Added from owner feedback on Support Build ch. 1: the prose read "clipped… uncanny valley." The named failure: AI prose spends maximum craft on every line — every sentence compressed, every paragraph ending on its snappiest unit, every speech quotable. No individual line is bad; the uniformity is the tell. Human writers spend attention unevenly, and the ordinary sentences are what make the good ones land.*

- **One snap per beat.** At most one compressed, quotable, or fragment-shaped unit per paragraph or dialogue exchange. If two land in a row, decompress one — usually the second.
- **Terse means fewer words, not sculpted words.** Clipped characters speak with lazy grammar — "sign this," "any questions?" — not metrically perfect beat-drops ("Contract."). A one-word dialogue closer is allowed about once per scene, and only if it's a word a person would actually say alone.
- **Personification-compression on a strict budget.** Giving objects and abstractions tidy agency ("the skill waits for a Gate," "the text knows what it's saying," "the room read her reading it") makes any line sound written. At most once per chapter, only when it clarifies a concrete action, never in a bit player's mouth.
- **Bit players speak plainly.** Not everyone is a phrasemaker. Ensemble texture comes from uneven polish — a tired clerk says "most of them read like tax law," not a balanced three-step epigram.
- **Let ordinary sentences be ordinary.** Carry transitions with physical action, not spoken gear-shifts. Deliver information in plain syntax. Slack is texture, not flab.
- **Numbers are stakes, not bookkeeping.** (Owner feedback, ch. 2 of Support Build: "AIs like to generate lots of logistics/accounting-ish prose, when really the reader doesn't care too much for the specific details.") State a figure when it changes what the reader wants or fears — the fatality stat, the days remaining, the debt that ends a storyline. When a scene needs "the office misbilled us and she caught it," show the catch, not the reconciliation: no itemised read-backs, no corrected-figure dialogue, no arithmetic transcripts. The exact numbers live in the ledgers so continuity stays auditable; the page gets the one number that matters.
- Worked example (owner-approved). Uncanny: *"Attend needs somewhere worth perceiving. It waits for a Gate. Contract."* Human: *"We're not testing that one in a gym. It can wait until there's a Gate around it." She went back to the table and dug the contract out from under the laptop. "Come sign before I remember what I'm agreeing to."* Same information; the performance is gone.

## Revision checklist

Run on every full draft:

1. **The calibration test:** any sentence that sounds profound but doesn't say what physically happened — rewrite it. Any paragraph that only adds mood — cut it or make it change the situation. Any dialogue explaining the theme — make the characters want something concrete and dodge each other instead.
2. Hunt every banned pattern and hard-avoid above; rewrite, don't delete-and-shrug.
3. Check every paragraph: does something happen, change, become clearer, become worse, or force a choice? If not, cut or rewrite.
4. Check the scene contract for each scene: want, opposition, turn. No turn, no scene.
5. Read dialogue per character — identifiable without tags? Check voice notes in `bible/characters.md`.
6. **Cadence audit (the slack rule):** scan for fragment-closers, one-word dialogue beat-drops, stacked compression, and personified abstractions. Keep the single best snap per beat; decompress the rest into ordinary sentences. If every line in a scene is landing, that's the problem — reintroduce slack.
7. Opening: in motion, no throat-clearing recap. Closing: a concrete hook (the ledger opens to the burned page), never "everything changed." Vary the closing note from the previous chapter's.
8. Cliffhanger honesty: the hook shows the actual problem, it doesn't withhold it.
9. Counterweight to steps that punch up prose ("rewrite the weakest sentences," "cut the flabbiest 5%"): these rules remove slack, and applied uniformly they produce the clipped, over-edited texture the slack rule exists to prevent. Cut flab, then check the page still has ordinary sentences in it.
