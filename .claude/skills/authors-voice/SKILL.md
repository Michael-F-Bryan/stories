# The author's voice

The goal is prose a reader would never clock as machine-written: concrete, confident, a little dry, with the craft hidden. These are working rules, not aspirations — the revision pass checks against them.

## Defaults

- **POV:** third person limited, one viewpoint character per chapter. Break this only as a deliberate, planned device.
- **Tense:** past.
- **Register:** plain and modern, flavored by the world's idiom — not faux-archaic. Characters in a fantasy world don't know they're in one.

## Principles

1. **Scene over summary.** If a moment matters, play it on the page with action and dialogue. Summarize only travel, routine, and the passage of time — and keep summaries short and voiced, not encyclopedic.
2. **Concrete over abstract.** Name the thing: not "the merchant's wares" but "salt cod and bolts of dyed wool." Sensory detail is rationed, though — one or two exact details beat five approximate ones.
3. **The viewpoint filters everything.** Description is what THIS character would notice, in words they'd use. A soldier and a cook walk into the same kitchen and see different rooms.
4. **Dialogue does work.** Every exchange advances plot, reveals character, or turns the scene — ideally two of the three. People interrupt, deflect, answer the question they wish they'd been asked. Attribution stays plain: *said*, *asked*, the occasional action beat. No "he smirked," "she quipped" merry-go-round.
5. **Distinct voices.** Each recurring character gets speech habits the reader could identify with the names removed: sentence length, vocabulary, what they joke about, what they refuse to say. Check `bible/characters.md` for each character's voice notes and keep them current.
6. **Emotion by implication.** Trust the reader. Show the clenched jaw or the too-careful pouring of tea; don't append "she was furious." Never explain a moment the scene already landed.
7. **Earn the numbers.** In progression stories, power growth is felt through what the character can now do that they couldn't in chapter one — shown in use, not recited in stat-blocks or self-narrated inventories.
8. **Chapters are shaped.** Open in motion — no throat-clearing recap. Close on a turn: a small resolution plus a new pressure. Vary the closing note; if the last three chapters ended on dread, end this one on warmth or a joke.

## Banned tics

These are the tells. The revision pass hunts them specifically:

- "A beat passed." / "Something shifted." / "She released a breath she didn't know she'd been holding."
- Triads used as a crutch: "X. Y. Z." rhythm more than once per chapter.
- One-sentence dramatic paragraphs more than once or twice per chapter.
- "couldn't help but", "found herself", "if she was being honest"
- Narrator telegraphing: "Little did he know," "It would be the last time," or any wink that the author knows the future.
- Every emotion getting a physical cliché: heart hammering, stomach dropping, blood running cold. One per chapter, if that.
- Over-qualified prose: "somewhat," "seemed to," "almost," "slightly" stacking up. Commit to the observation.
- Symmetrical "not X, but Y" constructions more than once per chapter.
- Characters constantly smirking, chuckling, raising eyebrows, or letting out low whistles.
- Em-dash pileups; more than a couple per page means the sentences need restructuring.

## Revision checklist

Run on every full draft, in this order:

1. Read the opening paragraph alone — does it start in motion, in voice?
2. Hunt every banned tic; rewrite, don't delete-and-shrug.
3. Read all dialogue aloud (mentally) per character — would the reader know who's speaking without tags?
4. Find the three weakest sentences in the chapter and rewrite them.
5. Check the closing beat — resolution plus pull, and a different note than the previous chapter's ending.
6. Wordcount sanity: cut the flabbiest 5% — there is always 5%.
