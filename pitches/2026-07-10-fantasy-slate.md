# Pitch slate — fantasy / progression

*2026-07-10. Four premises for the first serial. All spoiler-free: twists and endings get designed during planning, after greenlight, inside `plan/`.*

---

## 1. Compound Interest

**Logline:** In a city where every spell is a loan against the caster's own future, a junior clerk at the thaumic bank finds a debt in her name that she never signed.

**Blurb:** Magic in Coyle's Reach is not a gift; it is credit. Draw power today and the Ledger takes it back out of your tomorrows — years, luck, memory, whatever the contract names. The Bank of the Meridian underwrites it all, and Isa Voss is the most junior clerk in its audit hall: no magic of her own, just a genius for reading what a contract actually says instead of what it pretends to. When a routine audit turns up a lien against her own life — decades old, immaculately structured, unsigned — pulling that thread means learning to work the system from inside. Isa doesn't get stronger. She gets *leveraged*.

**Chapter engine:** Case-of-the-chapter — an audit, a default, a negotiation, a client who lied. Each closes a contract while the lien mystery compounds.

**Progression axis:** Financial-magical instruments as a skill tree: hedges, guarantees, transfers, liens. Power through structure, not firepower.

**Craft risks:** The magic-economics must stay rigorous enough to be satisfying without turning chapters into accounting lectures. Cleverness is hard to fake — the schemes have to actually work when the reader checks.

## 2. Palimpsest

**Logline:** Every night the city rearranges itself. The wayfinders' guild memorizes each new city and sells the routes — and its newest apprentice remembers every city there has ever been, which should be impossible.

**Blurb:** Nobody in Palimpsest owns a map for long. At dusk the bells ring everyone indoors, and by dawn the streets have been redealt: bridges landing in new districts, staircases ending in walls, a fishmonger's row where the courts stood. The wayfinders walk the new city at first light, memorize it, and sell safe passage until dusk wipes the slate again. Rell is a probationer with a flaw she's learned to hide — yesterday's city never fades for her, nor the one before, nor any of them. A thousand cities deep, she's starting to see what the shuffling has been hiding.

**Chapter engine:** Commission-of-the-chapter — a client, a route, a complication in a city nobody's walked before. The job resolves; what Rell noticed en route accumulates.

**Progression axis:** Guild ranks and route mastery on the surface; beneath it, learning to read the pattern in the shuffle — pattern-recognition as a power system.

**Craft risks:** A rearranging city flirts with unmappable-and-therefore-stakeless; the rules of the shuffle need firm edges. Heavy ledger burden — continuity across a thousand remembered cities is the point, so the bible has to be airtight.

## 3. Undermarch

**Logline:** The dungeon beneath the town is a dead god's memory, and the delvers looting its strata are grave-robbing a mind. One of them can read what the others merely sell.

**Blurb:** The god fell so long ago that the town on its grave forgot it was a grave. What everyone remembers is the Stair: floor after floor of the Undermarch, each one a preserved age of the god's remembering — an orchard frozen in someone's childhood summer, a war that never stops being lost, a library of rooms shaped like grief. Delve crews haul up crystallized memories to sell, and Ostry hauls with the worst of them. But where his crew sees loot, Ostry hears the memories speak — and lately, deeper down, some of them have started speaking *to* him, and they do not sound asleep.

**Chapter engine:** The delve — floor by floor, expedition by expedition, with topside politics (crews, buyers, the Delvers' Compact) between descents. The classic dungeon-crawl serial rhythm.

**Progression axis:** Depth as literal progression: deeper strata, stranger memories, and Ostry's reading growing from party trick to something the Compact would kill to control.

**Craft risks:** Dungeon serials live or die on floors feeling distinct rather than episodic wallpaper — every stratum has to characterize the god. Melancholy tone needs regular relief or it flattens.

## 4. Chosen Twice

**Logline:** She saved the world at nineteen and it wrung her out. Thirty years later the sword chooses again — a fourteen-year-old from her own village — and this time she knows exactly what the Church of the Blade does with its chosen.

**Blurb:** Every generation, the sword Vigil picks a hand, the Church picks up the child who owns it, and the world gets saved — everyone knows the story, because Maren Tal is how it ended last time. What the story leaves out is what it cost her, and what the Church quietly took while she was busy being a legend. Now she farms hops, aches when it rains, and hasn't touched a blade in decades. Then Vigil turns up in the hand of the miller's daughter, the Church's collectors are three days out, and Maren — burned-out, past her peak, and the only person alive who's read this story from the inside — volunteers as escort.

**Chapter engine:** A road serial: towns, escorts' trials, Church politics, training scenes where the mentor is relearning as much as teaching. Each stop resolves; the road and the collectors keep pressure on.

**Progression axis:** Inverted progression — the prodigy's raw gift is gone, so Maren rebuilds along a different axis: craft, guile, and what a veteran knows that a chosen one doesn't. The apprentice progresses classically alongside her.

**Craft risks:** Dual-protagonist balance — the girl can't become a prop, and Maren can't hog every resolution. The anti-Church throughline risks one-note cynicism without genuine believers who are right about some things.
