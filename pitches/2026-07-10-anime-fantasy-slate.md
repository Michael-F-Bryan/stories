# Pitch slate — anime-fantasy

*2026-07-10. Second slate, by request: four premises with the anime / battle-shonen / RoyalRoad energy — visible power systems, ranks and leagues, named techniques, rival escalation, duo banter, competence reveals. Spoiler-free as always: twists and endings get designed in `plan/` after greenlight.*

---

## 1. Support Build

**Logline:** In a world that ranks every awakened class by damage output, Ren draws the one class the leaderboards don't even track — and joins the worst-ranked raid team in the league to prove the meta wrong.

**Blurb:** When the Gates opened, humanity got the System, and the System got leaderboards. Strikers, casters, blade-dancers — everyone knows the S-tier classes, because the Gate League broadcasts them every weekend. Ren awakens as an *Attendant*: buffs, debuffs, aggro control, and a skill list the assessors openly laugh at. No guild wants a support. The only team that'll sign him is Grave Society — five members, one league win ever, currently ranked 214th of 215. But Ren has read his own skill descriptions more carefully than anyone in history, and some of these numbers do things nobody has bothered to test. The meta is about to get audited.

**Chapter engine:** Raid-of-the-chapter and league fixtures — each Gate or match is a tactical problem the team solves (or fails) on-screen, with team drama and league politics between fights.

**Progression axis:** Skill-synergy discovery and league climbing — the team rises through divisions as Ren finds what an Attendant can actually do, and rivals start copying the tech.

**Craft risks:** Fight clarity is everything — tactics must be honest and checkable, not retroactive genius. The underdog arc needs real losses, and teammates who are good at their own jobs, or it curdles into wish fulfillment.

## 2. Calamity, Housebroken

**Logline:** Hunter-candidate Mika fails her contract exam by accidentally bonding the sealed Calamity of the Western Reach — currently shaped like a fat, one-eyed housecat with opinions.

**Blurb:** Every hunter needs a bonded beast, and after three failed exams Mika would take anything with a pulse. What answers her contract circle is not the dormouse she paid for. It is ancient, it is furious about the seal, it can currently lift about half a rabbit, and the paperwork says it's hers. Now Mika has a license, a partner who remembers burning down kingdoms and can't open a jar, and a problem: seals wear thin, and the things that put the Calamity *in* the cat are still out there, keeping count. The Hunter Bureau ranks bonded pairs by verified kills. Mika and the cat start at rank: unranked.

**Chapter engine:** Bounty-of-the-chapter — hunts, escort jobs, exam retakes, Bureau inspections — carried by duo banter and the slow drip of what the cat used to be.

**Progression axis:** License ranks on paper; unsealing stages in truth — each recovered fraction of the cat's power has a cost and consequences, and attracts older attention.

**Craft risks:** Banter must never trivialise real danger (the voice notes are explicit) — the comedy needs to stop cold when stakes arrive. The cat's power has to stay mechanical and priced, or every fight becomes "the cat handles it."

## 3. Deadweight

**Logline:** Porters carry the rankers' gear up the Spire and swear an oath never to draw a weapon inside it. After six years and four hundred climbs, Edda knows every technique on the broadcast by heart — and floor 51 is about to make her break her oath on camera.

**Blurb:** The Spire showed up twenty years ago and the world reorganised around it: floors, gates, monsters, loot, and the Ascent League broadcasting every climb to a planet that can't look away. Rankers get the fame. Porters get the weight. Edda is the best porter on the lower floors — she knows which tiles lie, what the floor-9 wardens smell like before they spawn, and the exact footwork of every named technique she's spent six years watching from behind the baggage. Porters don't fight; that's the oath, and breaking it means the Spire itself takes notice. Then a sponsored climb goes wrong on 51, the rankers go down, and the only one left standing is the woman carrying their swords.

**Chapter engine:** Climb-by-climb ascent with the League machine around it — sponsors, broadcast narratives, ranker egos, and floors that each play by their own rules.

**Progression axis:** From baggage to ranker: fundamentals sharpened by ten thousand hours of watching, floor-knowledge no ranker bothered to learn, and whatever it means that the Spire noticed her.

**Craft risks:** Floors must feel distinct, not procedurally episodic. Her borrowed-by-observation skillset needs hard limits and visible costs, or she's a highlight reel. The oath-breaking consequence has to stay load-bearing, not forgotten.

## 4. The Thirteen Forms

**Logline:** Every sword school's named techniques cost the swordsman something — stamina, blood, years. The extinct Ruin School cost the most and beat everything. Orphan duellist Sen just inherited its manual, and the circuit is about to find out why it went extinct.

**Blurb:** In the duelling circuits of the Nine Cities, schools are public, techniques are named aloud before they're thrown, and the crowds know every form's price. Ember School burns stamina. Mirror School borrows your opponent's form and pays in blood. And the Ruin School — thirteen forms, no living practitioners — paid in things nobody writes down. Sen fights unranked exhibition bouts for food money until a dying stranger loses a duel on purpose, hands her a manual wrapped in oilcloth, and tells her to run. First Form costs a day of her life per cut. She's fifteen, broke, and very good at maths: that's a price she can pay for a while. The circuit has weight classes, rankings, season brackets — and now it has Sen.

**Chapter engine:** Duel-of-the-chapter through circuit seasons — public bouts with declared schools and visible prices, plus training arcs, patron politics, and people who recognise the Ruin forms and react badly.

**Progression axis:** Form by form up the Ruin School's ladder, each with a steeper price and a workaround to be earned — mastery here means learning what you can afford.

**Craft risks:** Costs must be tracked with total honesty — a promises-ledger natural — or the premise collapses. The escalating self-price trends grim; the circuit's showmanship and Sen's gallows pragmatism have to keep it fun.
