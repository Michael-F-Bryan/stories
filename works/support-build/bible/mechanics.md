# Mechanics — evergreen canon

*The power system. These rules are load-bearing: chapters must not contradict them, and schemes that exploit them must actually work when the reader checks.*

*Register warning: bible wit stays in the bible. Narration takes only the facts; the good lines belong to characters.*

## The System

- Arrived with the Gates in Year 0. Nobody knows what it is. It has never spoken, announced, updated, or explained. It does exactly three observable things: awakens people into **classes**, displays **panels**, and ranks **skills** with use.
- **Panels** are a private display only the user perceives — and *perceives* is the wrong word, which matters: **panels are System-to-user, not sensory.** They aren't seen with eyes or heard with ears; they're simply present to their owner. Nothing that shares, borrows, records, or mimics perception carries them — cameras don't film them, and perception-sharing skills don't transmit them. The only disclosure mechanisms are the user's own report and Authority **verification terminals** (assessment centres, league check-ins), which project a panel publicly and cannot be faked. People have tried, expensively.
- **CORE RULE — the text is exact.** System text means precisely what it says, does everything it says, and does nothing it does not say. It does not use words loosely. Twenty-two years of evidence: every apparent exception on record turned out to be a misreading. The public knows this rule the way the public knows compound interest: in theory, and not in practice.
- The System never patches. There are no updates — only discoveries. Skill behaviour "found" in Year 22 worked identically in Year 2; nobody had read carefully enough.

## Awakening and classes

- ~1 in 800 people, almost always between 16 and 20, correlated with cumulative aperture proximity. Late awakenings are rare and almost always Break-associated. The moment itself is quiet: a panel is simply there.
- The System assigns one **class** per person, permanently. ~140 classes are on the public registry. Frequency varies wildly: **Strikers** are nearly a third of all awakened; some classes have single-digit national counts.
- The Authority registry assigns each class a code on first recording (sequential within category, never reused). League culture sorts classes into six colloquial roles — striker, caster, warden, scout, mender, support — which is a broadcast convention, not a System category. The System has never named its own taxonomy.
- **Skills** are class-given, with exact text and a rank (I–X) that climbs with meaningful use. New skills appear at milestones the System does not announce in advance. Panels show skills only — no stats, no levels, no health bars. Bodies are still bodies: fitness, sleep, and training matter and are trained mundanely.
- **Techniques** are not skills. A technique is a human-developed application of a skill, named by players and broadcasters — [Ledger Cut], [Double Tap], [Curtain Call]. Skills are System-granted; techniques are craft and branding. Commentators name-drop techniques; the System has never acknowledged one.

## Focus

- Every skill activation draws on **Focus** — experienced as attention, not energy. Spending it feels like holding too many conversations at once.
- Focus is invisible: no bar, no number, no folk unit that survived contact with reality. Players learn their envelope by overdrawing it, which is why trainers exist, and talk about it in symptoms and fractions ("I was at half by the sluice").
- Overdraft symptoms in order: tunnel vision → flattened hearing → **grey-out** (skills refuse to fire) → in extremity, hours of clumsy, stupid exhaustion. Full recovery from a deep overdraft takes about a day; nothing known accelerates it except sleep and food.
- Sustained skills (auras, escorts, channelled effects) drain continuously; the drain scales with how much the effect is actually doing. An escort walking a flat corridor costs almost nothing; an escort through a rockslide bills accordingly.

## Parties

- A formal System structure, not a metaphor. Invite and acceptance are panel actions. Maximum eight slots; the League fields six.
- Party members perceive each other's names on HUD, a coarse condition light (steady / wavering / down), and can send **pings** — a directional attention-tap, like a tap on the shoulder from a distance.
- Many skills — most support-role skills — specify "party member" in their text and simply will not target outside the party. This is why support classes are close to untestable solo, and why so little is publicly known about the rare ones.

## Threat ("regard")

- Gaunts triage targets. The observed pattern is called **regard**: proximity, noise, damage dealt, and line of sight raise it; stillness and distance decay it. Wardens hold regard on purpose; everyone else manages it by instinct or dies educating their team.
- Regard is folk-science. The Authority publishes guidance; the forums publish better guidance; nobody has numbers, because the System doesn't share them.
- Common gaunt types and their regard habits: see `bible/bestiary.md`.

## Gates

- A **Gate** is an aperture — a standing tear, door-sized to hangar-sized — opening into an interior space (**the far side**) that is larger than the aperture and obeys its own layout: tunnels, forests, flooded galleries, whatever. Interiors are stable per-Gate and mapped by scout survey.
- Every Gate contains **gaunts** (the common word for the fauna; taxonomies are hobbyist) and exactly one **Core** — a mass, crystalline or grown-into-the-terrain, that the Gate is somehow *for*. Destroy the Core and the Gate closes within minutes; everything inside it that came from the far side stops mattering.
- **Grading** (E, D, C, B, A, S) is an Authority density assessment from aperture instrumentation, normally complete within 48 hours of detection; suspected high-grades get emergency grading in hours. It's good, not perfect. District Nine was a grading error.
- **Terms.** The term clock runs from *detection*, not grading. An uncleared Gate **Breaks** at the end of its term: the aperture ruptures wide and the far side empties into the street. Standard terms by grade: E ~90 days, D ~60, C ~40, B ~21, A ~10, **S ~72 hours**. An S-grade aperture is a national emergency by definition.
- **The escalation ladder.** Gates are offered by assignment priority down the league table. A Gate nobody claims has its bounty multiplier climb by the week; by the final term-week (**graveyard week**) the rates are the best risk-adjusted money in the division, which is a sentence only Grave Society has ever said out loud. Anything still unclaimed at term-minus-72-hours goes to the Authority's **Response Wing** — salaried, competent, chronically overcommitted. Breaks happen when grading was wrong, or the Wing was somewhere else. There are three or four Breaks in the Republic in a bad year, and every one is a public inquiry.
- **After a Break** the aperture stands open until the Core is destroyed — the Wing's grimmest work, clearing a Gate whose contents are already in the street. A closed Gate leaves a **scar**: an inert grey seam, instrument-noisy, harmless as far as anyone can prove. The fenced scar at the centre of Halden's Shelf is where District Nine's aperture stood. Ren grew up four streets from it.
- Casualties inside a Gate are real casualties. There is no respawn, no safe-log-out, and the League's insurance actuaries are the grimmest people alive.
- **Loot:** essence (Authority standard buyback, paid per grade-adjusted unit — a full final-week D-Gate clear typically grosses a team 15,000–25,000 marks before the League's 12% and expenses), materials (chitin, alloys, botanicals — private markets), and rarely **artifacts**: objects with their own System text. Artifact text follows the CORE RULE like everything else.

## The Gate League

- Charters teams, runs the season, owns the broadcast, and — critically — controls **assignment priority**: higher-ranked teams get first refusal on richer Gates. Sponsorship tiers key off division. Promotion is worth more than prize money.
- **Structure, Year 22:** 215 licensed teams. Eight **majors** (S-division) above four divisions: A (24), B (48), C (64), D (71). Roster: six fielded, eight registered max. **A team that cannot field six forfeits its season.**
- **Affiliates:** majors run feeder teams. An affiliate is licence-capped below its parent's division and may decline promotion, passing the slot down the table. Declining is legal, profitable, and despised. (Kestrel Nine, Vantage's affiliate, sits atop Division D on purpose.)
- **Eligibility:** league minimum age is 17 with a guardian counter-signature, 18 unrestricted. Rookie minimum deal, League-mandated: 2,600/month stipend plus an 8,000-mark signing bonus.
- **Season shape:** spring registration (a three-week window; rosters lock at the deadline) → **qualifiers** (each team clears assigned Gates on broadcast; scored on clear time, damage economy, control penalties, casualty flags) → split fixtures: head-to-head **twin-Gate races** (two instanced apertures of the same interior; first Core kill wins) → autumn playoffs and relegation.
- **Scoring rewards *clean*:** a slow no-injury clear can outscore a fast sloppy one. Casualty flags are score poison. This is canon and will matter tactically.
- **Relegation and licence review:** bottom eight of Division D at season's end face review — but review weighs the whole file: standings, *clear-completion rate, safety record, casualty flags*. A team that finishes low but completes every contract with zero casualties has, historically, kept its licence — which is the only reason Grave Society still has one. **As of this season that lifeline is narrowing:** the League added broadcast-value weighting to review criteria, and nobody's zero-casualty record photographs well at 214th. Failing review means demotion to freelance E-grade work, which pays like it sounds.
- **Duel-sims:** Authority-standard training instances with dummy gaunts, telemetry-scored; the League publishes division sim-ladders. Sim ratings are real skill measured with no cameras and no stakes — the gap between a player's sim rating and their fixture record is a stat GateWatch tracks gleefully (see: Theo Ganz).

## The Potential Index

- The League's headline metric for individual players, computed from output telemetry: damage coefficients, clear contributions, growth curve. The number that gets you recruited, sponsored, and shipped in fantasy leagues.
- **It is a League product, not a System product.** The System has never ranked a person.
- Support effects are largely invisible to output telemetry. Classes whose skills don't move the damage ledger assess as **UNINDEXED** — an official grade that formally means *unmeasured* and socially reads as *zero*. Almost nobody registers the distinction.

## The Attendant class

- Registry code **SVC-001** — the first service-category class ever recorded, Year 0. (Public trivia; registry nerds notice, nobody wonders.)
- Eleven recorded nationally in twenty-two years. Currently three: two employed as majors' valets and Ren. The valets are registered *staff*, not roster players — neither has ever been partied into a live Gate; a major doesn't spend a party slot on luggage. Practical upshot: **no one alive has public footage of Attendant skills used in a Gate.**
- **Ren's starting panel (canon text, exact):**
  - **[ESCORT] I** — *Designate a party member as your charge. While you escort your charge, they move 15% faster and cannot stumble.*
  - **[CARRY] I** — *Take up a willing party member's burden. What you carry costs them nothing.*
  - **[ATTEND] I** — *Wait upon your charge. While you attend, you perceive what your charge perceives.*
- Note: [ATTEND] shares *perception* — senses. It does not carry panels (see The System: panels are System-to-user, not sensory). The other boundary questions readers will ask have canonical answers ruled in advance; chapters establish them on-page, and established rulings are recorded in `ledgers/` as published canon.

## Canon skill text — Grave Society roster

*Exact text for each teammate's core skills, so training scenes and Gate clears never improvise wording. Ranks shown are current at story start; rank movement is ledger business. Kits are 2–3 skills here; classes have more, added only via the loop when needed.*

**Cass Ferro — Bulwark (warden):**
- **[HOLD FAST] VII** — *Plant your feet. While you hold your ground, you cannot be forced from it.* (She can be hurt where she stands; she cannot be moved. Sustained Focus drain scales with what's pushing.)
- **[SHELTER] V** — *Raise your shield over another. Harm that would reach them reaches you instead.* (Full harm, untranslated. Her body is the mitigation.)
- **[DRAW REGARD] VI** — *Demand attention. Enemies near you regard you first.*

**Theo Ganz — Edgerunner (striker):**
- **[EDGE] IV** — *Your next step is faster than it should be.* (Burst acceleration; per-step Focus cost.)
- **[FOLLOW-THROUGH] IV** — *A cut that lands feeds the next. The chain grows while you do not miss.* (Momentum stacking. A miss — or a freeze — drops the chain to zero. His camera problem is mechanically expensive.)
- **[SIDESTEP] III** — *Once per engagement, you are not where the blow lands.* (His panic button. What counts as an "engagement" is a live table argument.)

**Juno Vale — Pyrokinetic (caster):**
- **[KINDLE] V** — *Call heat to your hands. It grows while you feed it.* (The feeding is Focus. Her overcasting is written into her own skill text: it grows while she feeds it, and she feeds it.)
- **[SHAPE FLAME] IV** — *Fire you called obeys you.*
- **[BANK] II** — *Set a called fire aside for later.* (Stored charge. Rank II because she never saves.)

**Pip Andrade — Tracker (scout):**
- **[MARK] IV** — *Mark what you see. Your party sees your marks.*
- **[READ GROUND] V** — *The ground tells you what passed, and when.*
- **[LOOSE] III** — *An arrow you loose flies where you looked.* (Where she *looked*, not where she's looking — pre-aimed release; the wording rewards her memory.)

**Doc Amara — Chaplain (mender):**
- **[MEND] VI** — *Close what is open. The body pays half; you pay the rest.* (Healing is a shared bill. He rations himself accordingly.)
- **[TRIAGE] V** — *Know at a glance who is worst.*

## Rules for writing mechanics on the page

- Every exploit must survive a careful reader: quote the text, show the reading, show the cost.
- Skills have prices — Focus, positioning, opportunity cost. A fight won by skill-text cleverness should still cost something.
- Lore arrives through pressure (a fight, an argument, a price), never as a systems lecture. Pip can recite numbers *because that's characterisation*.
