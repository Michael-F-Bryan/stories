Ren’s new harness had two steel rings at the hips, four adjustment straps, and a warning label long enough to cover most of his ribs.

He stood on the gym mat while Juno pulled the waist strap tight.

“Can you breathe?” she asked.

“Yes.”

“Can you breathe comfortably?”

“No.”

She loosened it one notch. “Better?”

“Yes.”

Doc looked over from the folding table. “Now hang him from it.”

Theo clipped a training rope to both rings and fed it through a pulley on the wall. Ren leaned back until the harness took his weight. The broad belt pressed into his hips instead of dragging his shoulders forward.

His palms had new skin under the dressings, and the League doctor had cleared him that morning. The doctor had also told him to stop if the light streak returned in his vision. Ren had repeated that part to Doc before Doc could ask.

Theo hauled on the other end of the rope. Ren’s boots slid half a metre across the mat.

“Useful,” Ren said.

“It needs leg loops,” Doc said.

“It has leg loops.”

“It needs better ones.”

Juno wrote that on the receipt and put it beside the new stretcher invoice. Their first upgrades were spread around the gym: the harness, replacement arrows, clean rope, two waterproof coal tins, and a stretcher that rolled straight. Theo’s serviced swords lay beside his kit. Cass’s repaired shield stayed against the wall while Doc’s two-week restriction held.

Cass stood at the whiteboard with her left arm out of the sling. She could move it below shoulder height, but Doc had not changed the two-week restriction. The Core hammer was on the floor by her right boot.

A League producer had joined them by video call. She shared a screen showing the second-round broadcast notes for Morrow Yard.

“The booth needs names for the Attendant applications,” she said. “Human technique names, not System skills. When Ren calls one, the graphics desk can tag the footage.”

Ren checked the screen. Two empty boxes sat under his name. “Does the name change what we call inside the Gate?”

“It becomes the call if you want the tag to land at the right time.”

“Then it has to be clear to us before it is clear to the booth,” Cass said.

“That’s fine. I need repeatable words.”

Theo lay on the mat beside the rope pulley. “Call Carry ‘The Passenger’. They already paid for the graphic.”

Nobody answered. After a few seconds, he sat up.

“All right. Bad idea.”

Pip was reading the public registry entry on her tablet. “SVC-001 means Attendant was the first service-category class recorded. It doesn’t mean it was ranked first.”

The producer looked interested. “Can I use that?”

“It’s public.”

Ren looked at his assessment printout on the table. The code had been there every time he read it. Nobody at the assessment centre had explained it.

Cass tapped the first empty box on the broadcast sheet. “Carry transfers a load. Call it Load Shift.”

The producer typed **[LOAD SHIFT]**.

“For Escort with a rope route, Guide Line,” Pip said. “The rope makes it visible.”

Ren thought about the skill text and the new harness. “Only use that name when there is an actual line. Escort by itself is still Escort.”

The producer added **[GUIDE LINE]** to the second box.

Theo got to his feet. “Our techniques sound like warehouse procedures.”

“We are going to a warehouse,” Juno said.

Morrow Yard’s aperture had opened inside an abandoned rail-maintenance depot at the eastern edge of Halden. The far side was another maintenance yard, although its tracks ran through a roofed hall much wider than the building around the Gate. Authority scouts had mapped the Core in a signal cabin above the far wall.

The latest survey showed a derailed freight car had crushed the footbridge to the cabin. A maintenance trench still reached a shaft beneath it. That was the route Cass planned to use, and the reason Ren’s new harness was already part of the Gate kit.

The survey listed two common D-grade gaunts. Skitters hunted movement in packs. Bell jellies floated near the roof and followed sound. A jelly burst when struck, throwing a concussive ring across the space around it.

Pip put the survey image on the whiteboard. “There are thirty-one bell jellies in the last count. We don’t fight under them. We move them with sound before we cross each bay.”

“What do they do when a skitter pack is making the noise?” Ren asked.

“They drift towards it.”

“And if one bursts above the pack?”

“The ring staggers everything. It won’t kill the skitters.”

“Theo and Juno can do that part,” Cass said. “I’m not drawing regard without the shield. Nobody builds a plan around me stopping a charge.”

Theo stopped playing with the pulley rope. Cass erased her name from the front position on the board and wrote his there.

Cass pointed to the formation. “Theo leads. Pip works behind him. Juno holds the left. Ren stays where he can see the route and the camera operator stays with Doc. I am inside the formation with the hammer.”

“What happens if the map is wrong?” Juno asked.

“We stop and read the route we have.”

Cass drew one line from the aperture to the signal cabin. “This is a D-grade with four days on the term. We clear it cleanly and leave.”

---

The Morrow Yard aperture opened between two inspection pits in the abandoned depot. Weeds grew through the concrete outside, and every rail ended at a locked roller door.

Inside, the rails continued farther than Ren could see.

The far-side hall smelled of old grease and rain on hot metal. Parallel tracks ran beneath a high roof crossed by maintenance cranes. Freight cars stood on some of the lines with their doors open. Others had tipped against the platforms or sunk axle-deep into cracked concrete. Pale bell jellies floated near the roof trusses, each one trailing a fringe of clear tendrils.

The League camera operator crossed last and took the place beside Doc. Cass pointed at the floor in front of him.

“You stop there when we stop. Do not walk backwards.”

“I watched the Stacks replay,” he said.

“Then you know why.”

Pip raised one hand. Everyone went quiet.

A group of four jellies near the first crane had begun drifting towards the camera operator as he shifted his boots. Their translucent bells contracted at each scrape.

Pip spoke softly. “Sound brings them closer. They burst if they touch a hard edge, and the ring reaches about eight metres. That first group is moving at less than walking speed.”

Theo looked up. “Can we whisper for the whole Gate?”

“No,” Cass said. “We make noise where we want them.”

The first bay held a skitter pack under a freight wagon. Ren saw nine sets of legs between the wheels. The creatures had not moved towards the team yet. Pip marked each one, and nine red shapes appeared low to the ground.

A steel lunch tin lay on the platform beyond the wagon. Pip drew an arrow, looked at the tin, and turned her bow towards the roof.

“[LOOSE] sends the arrow where I looked,” she said. “I can hit the tin without aiming at it again, and the jellies will cross over the pack.”

“Do it,” Cass said.

Pip fired. The arrow travelled above the wagon, dropped behind it, and struck the tin where she had looked. The clang carried through the hall.

All four jellies changed direction.

They floated over the marked skitters. One jelly brushed a crane rail and burst.

The concussive ring hit the wagon roof with a flat boom. Dust fell from the trusses. The skitters rolled out from under the wagon, legs folding and reopening as they tried to stand.

Theo ran in before they recovered. His first cut landed through the neck of the nearest one, and Follow-Through carried him into the next. Juno kept her fire low and used short bursts when a skitter moved outside his chain. Pip shot the two that reached the platform edge.

Cass stayed behind Theo with the hammer ready in her right hand. She did not use Draw Regard.

Ren watched the camera position, the jellies overhead, and the route to the next bay. He had no skill running yet.

The ninth skitter broke away from Theo and sprinted towards Doc.

Ren designated Theo as his Escort charge.

“Theo, Escort on you. Left rail.”

Theo changed direction. The skill added speed to his next step and kept his feet under him when he crossed an oil patch beside the track. He reached the skitter before it reached Doc and cut it down near the inspection pit.

The camera operator kept the lens on both of them.

“Was that Guide Line?” he asked.

“No line,” Ren said. “That was Escort.”

Cass looked back. “Keep moving.”

They passed through two more bays by sending sound ahead of them. Pip struck hanging tools and empty drums with arrows. The jellies followed each noise, leaving open lanes under the trusses. The team moved only after Pip checked that the lane was clear.

At the third bay, Doc’s pack began catching on the narrow gap between a freight car and the platform wall. He had to turn sideways, which pushed the camera operator towards the rail.

Ren asked for the burden. Doc agreed.

“Load Shift on Doc.”

Ren used Carry. The pack stayed on Doc’s shoulders, but its weight settled across Ren’s back. Doc stopped fighting the straps and moved through the gap without turning.

A blue graphic appeared on the camera operator’s return screen: **[LOAD SHIFT] — REN OKAFOR**.

“Graphics got it,” the operator said.

Ren ducked under a bent pipe. “Watch the gap.”

The signal cabin stood at the far end of the hall on a concrete tower above four switching tracks. A derailed freight car lay where the survey said it would, across two tracks with one end against the tower.

Its underframe had torn the bridge from the wall, leaving the cabin door five metres above the floor. A maintenance ladder ran down the tower beside it, but the bottom section had been folded under the car.

Pip checked the concrete around the wheels. “It hasn’t moved since the survey. The trench should still be open.”

“Can we climb the car?” Theo asked.

“The roof is under three jellies.”

Juno looked up at them. “I can move them with a heat pop against the far wall, but the sound will draw anything else in the cabin.”

Cass walked around the end of the car and found an inspection trench between the rails. The trench passed beneath the crushed bridge and ended at a maintenance shaft under the signal cabin. Iron rungs climbed the shaft wall to a hatch in the cabin floor.

The trench floor was dry, though water coated the iron rungs.

Ren released Load Shift before he entered. Doc took the pack’s weight back and adjusted the straps.

Theo climbed into the trench first. Pip followed and checked the shaft. The first three rungs held. The fourth turned under her hand.

“There are two loose rungs and one missing near the top,” she said. “The shaft is about four metres.”

Cass looked at her left shoulder. “I can climb with one hand. I cannot catch myself with that arm.”

Ren uncoiled the new rope.

“I can belay from below,” he said. “Escort keeps your feet from slipping while there is a rung under them. The rope catches you if a rung comes away.”

Cass checked the buckles on his harness. “Who goes first?”

“Theo. He sets the top line. Everyone else goes before you and me.”

Theo climbed through the floor hatch and cleared the cabin. He passed the rope through an old lifting eye above the hatch and dropped the end back down the shaft. Pip went next, then Juno, Doc, and the camera operator.

Ren clipped the belay to both rings on his harness and lowered the Core hammer separately. He made a seat sling for Cass from a second length of rope, checked the knot, and clipped the main line to it.

Cass checked his knot before she stepped into the shaft.

Ren took a wide stance at the bottom. The harness sat low across his hips. He designated Cass as his Escort charge.

“Guide Line on Cass.”

The blue technique graphic appeared again on the return screen above them.

Cass climbed. Ren watched her right boot, called the loose rung, and kept the rope short without lifting her off the ladder. When her foot reached the missing rung, he directed it to the edge of the wall bracket instead.

The bracket shifted under her boot.

Escort kept her foot from sliding off what remained. The bracket itself tore out of the concrete a second later.

Cass dropped half a metre. The rope snapped tight through the lifting eye and pulled Ren forward. The harness drove into his hips. He bent his knees and held the line while Cass caught the next rung with her right hand.

“I have it,” she said.

Ren checked his vision. There was no dark rim or light streak. “Keep climbing.”

She reached the hatch and Theo pulled her into the signal cabin.

Ren unclipped from the rope and climbed last.

The Core had grown through the signal desk. It was smaller than the knot in the Stacks crane cab, a dark glassy mass threaded through old switch levers. Six skitters lay dead around it. Theo had cleared the cabin while the others climbed.

Juno stood beside the desk with one hand over a coal tin. “The casing is steel. I can heat one side so the bolts move, but I don’t want to feed the whole desk.”

“Short burst,” Cass said.

Theo picked up the Core hammer. The camera operator moved to the doorway and kept the whole desk in frame.

Juno heated the bolt line until the metal changed colour. Pip marked the weakest seam. Theo struck it with the hammer, opening the casing without touching the Core. He changed his grip and hit the dark mass once.

The Core split.

Every bell jelly in the hall began to sink. Their clear bodies folded when they touched the floor. The light beyond the cabin windows flattened as the Gate started to close.

Cass looked at Ren. “Take us back the same way.”

They descended on the same rope. Ren used Guide Line for Cass again and kept more weight on the line at the gap where the bracket had torn out. At the first bay, the jellies lay across the concrete like empty sacks. The team crossed the aperture with the camera still running.

The status board changed to CLEAR when Cass came through last.

Their clear time was eighteen minutes and twenty-two seconds, without casualty flags or control penalties. The technique tags appeared beneath Ren’s name before the replay began.

When the second-round totals settled that evening, Grave Society ranked thirty-first out of seventy-one.

They had qualified for the split.

---

The League held the regional split draw two days later in a conference room above Halden Arena.

Grave Society occupied one table near the back. Cass wore the sling again because Doc had seen her carry the hammer out of Morrow Yard. Theo sat beside Ren and kept his phone face-down. The Morrow replay had replaced the interview freeze on most of the afternoon sports feeds, although the older clip was still everywhere else.

Kestrel Nine took the table in front of them.

Marc Osei came in last with a Kestrel jacket over his training clothes. He was taller than Ren expected and carried a folded scouting staff under one arm. Theo had described him often enough that Ren recognised him before the name appeared on the seat card.

Marc stopped beside Grave Society’s table.

“Cass,” he said.

“Marc.”

“I saw the C-grade clear. Congratulations.”

“Thank you.”

Marc looked at Theo. “You all right?”

Theo turned his phone over once, then put it face-down again. “I’m here.”

Marc nodded and turned to Ren. “You filled the slot.”

“Yes.”

“I’m glad they found someone.”

“So am I,” Ren said.

Dree Vann called Marc back to the Kestrel table. Marc put the scouting staff under his chair and sat with his new team.

The draw began with the top eight seeds locked into separate groups. Fixtures appeared on the main screen one at a time. Brickyard Union drew Vesper Youth. Sunder Bros. drew the Palisades. Coldharbour’s travelling supporters cheered from the public section when their club drew a Corvel team.

Grave Society’s name appeared on the left side of the screen.

Kestrel Nine appeared on the right.

The room turned towards the two Halden tables. Dree Vann smiled for the cameras. Marc read the pairing and looked back at Theo.

“See you at the Gate,” he said.

Theo nodded. “Yeah.”

The first split fixture was Kestrel Nine against Grave Society.
