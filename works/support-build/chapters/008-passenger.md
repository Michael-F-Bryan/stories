The League doctor shone a light into Ren’s left eye and told him to follow it.

He did. The dark rim had gone from his vision, but the light left a pale streak wherever it moved. Rain ticked on the roof of the medical tent. Outside, port staff were taking down the barriers around the aperture site.

“Any nausea?” the doctor asked.

“No.”

“Headache?”

“A small one.”

“Hands?”

Ren held them out. Both palms had been cleaned and covered with square dressings. The shaking had stopped sometime after the ambulance left.

The doctor checked his pupils again. “No skill use until tomorrow, and no training before I clear you in the morning. Your club medic has already told me you understand that.”

“He made me say it aloud.”

“Good man.”

Doc sat on the next folding bed with his knitting in his lap. He had not made a stitch since Ren entered the tent.

“Can he go?” Doc asked.

“He can sit somewhere else.”

“That will do.”

Ren pulled on his damp jacket. The sleeve stuck to his forearm where the hook had clipped him, though the bruise there did not need treatment. Across the tent, Cass was arguing with a second doctor about her shoulder. The doctor wanted an X-ray at the port clinic. Cass wanted the official result first.

The doctor won because Doc joined him.

Theo, Juno, and Pip waited under the League awning beside a bank of screens. Juno had found six paper cups of tea and was carrying all of them in a cardboard tray that had started to soften in the rain. Pip still had the stopwatch looped around her thumb. Theo had changed into a dry League jacket with the sponsor patches taped over.

“Where’s Cass?” he asked.

“X-ray.”

“Good. She was going to pretend bones become optional after a clear.”

“She said it might be a bruise,” Pip said.

“She also tried to swing a hammer with it.”

Juno gave Ren a cup. “There’s sugar in that because you look terrible, and Doc said food, and tea is nearly food if you make it badly enough.”

“It is very bad,” Ren said after tasting it.

“Thank you.”

The largest screen showed the qualifier table. Grave Society’s row sat near the bottom with a grey WAITING tag beside it. Most teams had already completed their assigned Gates. Twelve rows still showed LIVE or PENDING.

This was the first of two qualifier rounds. Every Division D team would clear one more Gate before the split fixtures began.

The team’s entry footage played in a small window to the right. Six skitters rushed Cass’s line. Theo cut through them while the camera stayed behind her shield. The replay ended before the junction.

Pip watched the table rather than the footage. “The League has the clear time. They’re waiting on control penalties, medical flags, and damage economy.”

“Ilyas went out in an ambulance,” Ren said. “How is that not a medical flag?”

“He isn’t rostered to us.” Pip frowned at the wording beneath the table. “The team gets a camera-loss penalty. The casualty review is separate because he was League staff.”

Theo looked over. “That sounds like somebody found the right box on a form.”

“It means his injury doesn’t count against our score,” Pip said. “I’m explaining it, not agreeing with it.”

A League producer came through the awning with a tablet under one arm. She had been at the gym on media day, though today her hair was stuck flat to her forehead and she had lost the patience she brought to training.

“Ilyas is stable,” she said before anyone asked. “He has a torn ligament in the knee and they’re keeping him overnight because of the water he breathed. He was awake when the ambulance arrived.”

Juno put the tea tray down on the floor. “Can we see him?”

“Not tonight. His family are there.”

“Did they get the camera back?” Ren asked.

The producer glanced at him. “The Gate closed before the retrieval crew could enter. We received the feed until the Core broke, but the lens stayed underwater. It shows the junction floor, three sodden, and a lot of black water. Audio is mostly interference.”

“So nobody saw the crane,” Theo said.

“Nobody outside the Gate.”

The result screen chimed.

Grave Society’s WAITING tag changed to CLEAR VERIFIED. A row of figures appeared beside it: clear time, damage economy, control penalties, casualty flags. Their clear time sat deep in the red. Damage economy was green. Control penalties showed one for the lost camera. Casualty flags showed zero.

The provisional placement at the far right changed twice while Ren watched. It stopped at thirty-fourth.

Pip read the scoring breakdown from top to bottom. “We lost almost everything available for time. Damage economy is fourth in the division so far. Zero casualty flags moved us above sixteen faster clears.”

“Thirty-fourth,” Juno said. “We were predicted seventy-first.”

“Twelve teams haven’t finished.”

“Pip.”

“Yes?”

“Let us have thirty-fourth for one minute.”

Pip checked her stopwatch and pressed the button.

The team stood under the awning and watched Grave Society’s name in the middle of the table. Ren had expected the result to feel louder. Instead, staff kept carrying cables past them, rain kept blowing under the awning, and his tea remained bad.

Theo bumped Ren’s elbow with his own. “Passenger did all right.”

“What passenger?”

Theo pointed to the replay screen.

The League package had moved on from the opening pull. It showed Ilyas stepping into the flood, the camera going under, then the six members of Grave Society emerging through the aperture forty-one minutes later. A graphic listed each player’s measurable clear contribution.

Cass had regard held, damage absorbed, and Core damage. Theo, Juno, and Pip had damage. Doc had medical intervention. Ren’s line read NO RECORDED OUTPUT.

Kef’s voice came over the awning speakers. “You have to ask what they carried the Attendant for. We saw the pack trick in training, Ambrose, but inside the Gate he was a passenger on the cleanest ugly clear of the round.”

Ambrose Tan took a few seconds before answering. “We lost their interior feed after the seventh minute. Grave Society entered as six and exited as six, with the camera operator alive. The official telemetry does not tell us how they managed that.”

“It tells us the rookie did zero damage.”

“He is an Attendant.”

“Exactly.”

The clip restarted. This time the lower caption read GRAVE SOCIETY’S PASSENGER CLEAR.

Ren drank the tea because it gave him something to do with his mouth.

Juno stepped in front of the screen. “Okay, so we can turn that off, or I can find the cable and make it stop being a cable.”

“It’s on every monitor,” Pip said.

“I can be ambitious.”

Theo had gone still beside Ren. He was looking past the screen at the producer.

“You have interview cameras here,” he said.

The producer shifted the tablet to her other arm. “The booth wants two players for a result reaction. Cass already agreed before entry.”

“I’ll do it.”

Ren looked at him. “You don’t have to.”

“I know.” Theo pulled the borrowed jacket straight. “That’s why it counts as volunteering.”

The joke came out at his normal speed. He was already walking towards the interview mark.

The producer looked from Theo to Ren. “Both of you, then. The Attendant is the story.”

Ren followed because saying no would leave Theo alone in front of the lens.

The interview position was ten metres from the awning, under a portable light that turned the rain white. A camera operator clipped a microphone to Ren’s jacket. Another fitted one to Theo. Behind them, the aperture had collapsed to a wet grey seam on the concrete.

The producer stood beside the camera and counted them in with her fingers.

Ambrose appeared on a return monitor. He asked Theo about the first C-grade clear in Grave Society’s history.

Theo looked at the lens. “We took too long, but the route back came apart after we turned around. We got Ilyas out and cleared because there wasn’t another way to leave.”

His answer was quick, but complete. Ren could see the effort in the way Theo kept both hands hooked in the pockets of the borrowed jacket.

Ambrose asked Ren what an Attendant contributed once the broadcast camera was lost.

“I shared our scout’s sight during the underwater rescue,” Ren said. “I escorted her across the damaged racking, carried the Focus cost of Cass’s Hold Fast at the sluice, and escorted Theo onto the crane roof. I stopped when tunnel vision hit and held the stretcher by hand.”

The producer’s eyes moved to the tablet. She had not known most of that.

Kef leaned towards his desk on the monitor. “The telemetry has no damage or healing output for you. Do you think the League needs a separate measure for support work?”

“Yes,” Ren said.

Kef waited for more.

“That is my answer.”

Theo laughed once beside him. It sounded almost normal.

Then Kef asked, “So was the passenger graphic wrong?”

Theo took his hands out of his pockets.

“He got us through the—”

The red tally light above the camera came on.

Theo stopped speaking.

Rain struck the portable light and ran down the black casing. The camera operator waited. Ambrose tried the question again, slower this time.

Theo kept looking at the lens. His mouth opened, but no sound came out.

Ren could hear the crowd behind the barrier and a forklift reversing near the League trucks. He wanted to answer for Theo. Doing that would turn Theo’s silence into part of the segment, so he stayed where he was.

Five seconds passed. Then ten.

The producer lowered her counting hand.

Cass walked into the light wearing a sling under her jacket. “The graphic is wrong,” she said. “End the interview.”

The camera operator looked at the producer. She cut the feed.

Theo turned away before the tally light had gone dark. He walked past the awning and kept going towards the van.

Cass watched him leave. “Pip, go with him.”

Pip handed Juno the stopwatch and followed.

The producer removed Ren’s microphone. “That will still go out with a delay.”

“Then use the first answer,” Cass said.

“I don’t control the booth.”

“Find whoever does.”

The producer went.

Cass took the microphone off her own jacket and put it in the operator’s hand. The sling held her left arm close to her chest.

“Crack?” Ren asked.

“No. Deep bruise and a strained joint. Two weeks without the shield.”

“That sounds worse than you said.”

“It is why doctors take pictures.”

She looked towards the van. Theo had reached it, with Pip several steps behind him.

“Go and sit down,” Cass said. “We have payment paperwork in twenty minutes.”

---

The League payment statement reached Cass’s tablet before they left the port.

She read it from the front passenger seat while Juno drove. Doc sat behind her with the sling instructions open on his phone. Theo had taken the back row. He had said he was fine once, and nobody had asked again.

“The clear purse and Core buyback have cleared,” Cass said. “The League took its share before payment. We can cover rent, pay everyone’s Gate share, replace the damaged kit, and spend a little on upgrades.”

Juno slowed for a red light. “How little?”

“Enough to buy something useful. You are not getting a flamethrower.”

“I wasn’t going to ask for a flamethrower.”

“You were breathing like you were going to ask for a flamethrower.”

“That seems medically unreliable.”

Doc looked up from the instructions. “It was diagnostic.”

Pip had the provisional table open on her phone. Grave Society had slipped to thirty-sixth after another clear. “A top-half qualifier placement would move assignment priority enough to give us three D-grade choices for the second round.”

“Would,” Theo said from the back. “There are still teams running.”

It was the first thing he had said since the interview.

Pip turned in her seat. “Yes.”

“Then stop refreshing it.”

She put the phone face-down on her knee.

Ren’s own phone vibrated. Bea had sent him a screenshot of the table with a circle around Grave Society’s row.

`34 when i took this`

A second message followed.

`the tv man is an idiot`

Ren typed back with the edge of his thumb because the dressings made both hands clumsy.

`36 now`

Three dots appeared, vanished, then returned.

`still wrong about you`

He locked the phone without answering.

At the gym, they spread the damaged equipment over the mats. One shield strap had torn near the buckle. Theo’s sword needed a new edge where it had levered the brake stop. Pip had five arrows left from the quiver she carried inside. The stretcher frame had a bend near the front wheel, and two lengths of Ren’s rope were stiff with warehouse water and blood.

Ren’s Gate share arrived while Cass checked the stretcher. He opened the Recovery Trust page, paid 1,900 marks against the next quarter, and saved the receipt before the app could log him out. He sent the receipt to Bea.

She replied before he put the phone away.

`read it twice?`

`three times`

On the whiteboard, Cass listed shield repair, replacement arrows, sword servicing, a new stretcher, rope, gloves, and a proper lifting harness for Ren.

Juno added waterproof coal tins beneath it and started another line.

Cass took the marker from her.

“I was going to write labels.”

“Use mine.”

Theo sat on the edge of the ring and cleaned his second sword. His phone kept vibrating in his kit bag. On the fourth buzz, he got up, switched it off, and went back to the blade.

Ren found his printed assessment panel in the pocket of his locker. The paper had been folded enough times that the creases had begun to split.

Cass stood beside him. “Read me the whole panel.”

He looked at the copy in his hand. “You read it at the tryout.”

“Read it again.”

The others were arguing over whether Pip needed a second quiver or simply more arrows. Ren turned so his body blocked the locker from the room.

“Attendant,” he read. “Registry code SVC-001. Potential Index: unindexed.”

Cass waited.

“[Escort] I. Designate a party member as your charge. While you escort your charge, they move fifteen per cent faster and cannot stumble.”

“Next.”

“[Carry] I. Take up a willing party member’s burden. What you carry costs them nothing.”

“Next.”

“[Attend] I. Wait upon your charge. While you attend, you perceive what your charge perceives.”

Cass looked at that line for long enough that Ren checked whether she had heard him.

“What are you looking for?” he asked.

Before she answered, the gym tablet sounded from the desk.

Pip reached it first. “Second qualifier notice.”

Everyone turned towards her.

She read the assignment twice. “Morrow Yard. D-grade. The term expires in four days.”

Cass took the tablet from her and checked the acceptance deadline.

“How much?” Theo asked.

Pip named the graveyard-week bounty.

Juno looked at the equipment list on the whiteboard. “That buys the harness.”

“It buys more than the harness,” Cass said.

She accepted the Gate.
