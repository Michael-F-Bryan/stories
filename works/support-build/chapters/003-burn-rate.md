# 3 — Burn Rate

On Sunday, day two, Cass moved training to the rail easement by the river, because the gym had run out of distance.

The morning question was range. Pip walked away from Ren along the gravel with her hands in her pockets while he held the escort and counted her steps, and the answer turned out to be generous and then suddenly not. Sixty metres in the open and the skill held like she was standing next to him. Then a freight crew shunted a container across the sightline, and the escort died before Ren had finished thinking the word *wall*.

"So it's not distance," he reported, when Pip trudged back. "As long as I can track you, you're covered. The moment I can't, you're not. Open ground is cheap. Clutter is the enemy."

"The Stacks is clutter," Pip said. "The Stacks is the most clutter in the division."

*Of course it is,* Ren thought. He was starting to understand how this team's luck was distributed.

Juno had brought breakfast for six in a cooler bag, because apparently that was a thing she did on Sundays, and they ate it sitting on the container that had killed the escort. Nobody talked tactics for ten whole minutes. It was the first time Ren heard Doc laugh — a short rusty sound, at a story about Theo's first licence exam — and he caught himself filing the sound away like it was data. Maybe it was. A week ago he'd been a rumour with a printout. Now he had a seat on the cooler bag rotation.

---

Tuesday was the media obligation.

The League sent a two-person crew for qualifier-week profiles: a shoulder-rig operator and a producer with a checklist. "Twenty minutes of training footage, one line from the captain, we're gone by ten," the producer said. "You won't even notice us."

Theo noticed them.

Ren had spent four days learning what Theo looked like at full speed — the chain of cuts flowing one into the next, each landed hit feeding the momentum of the following one, a man playing downhill. With the lens on him, Ren watched it happen in reverse. First cut clean. Second cut a half-beat late. On the third, Theo's eyes went to the camera instead of the target dummy, the chain died, and the combination that he could do asleep came apart into three separate movements that had never met each other.

Nobody said anything. That was somehow the loudest part — Juno launched into a story about flood maps at double volume, Pip suddenly needed to re-fletch arrows, and Cass walked over to the producer and gave her one captain-line early, standing exactly where the shoulder-rig had to turn away from the floor to frame her.

Theo put the training sword back on the rack like it was made of eggshell. He came and sat next to Ren on the bench.

"Go on," he said. "Ask."

"I wasn't going to ask."

"Everyone asks eventually." Theo watched the producer pack up her checklist. "You know what the worst part is, Butler? I'm *good*. Ratings say top fifty. Empty gym, I'll take apart anyone in this division." He bounced his heel off the floor, twice, fast. "Forty million people watch this league. I picked the one job where the room is never empty."

Ren thought about the assessment hall, the recruiters looking at his projected panel and glancing away in one motion.

"I don't have any advice," he said.

"Good. I'd have hated that." Theo got up, rolled his shoulders, and went back to the dummies, and his chain ran clean for an hour, because by then the camera was gone.

That evening Cass rewrote the Stacks roles on the whiteboard. She didn't announce it and she didn't explain it, but when the new diagram went up, Theo's opening position was on the far side of the formation from where the broadcast rig would enter. Theo studied it for a while.

"Solid plan," he said, to nobody in particular.

---

Wednesday, day five, they burned Ren to the edge on purpose.

It was Cass's experiment, but Doc ran it, which told Ren something about how the team ranked its dangers. The question on the whiteboard: *what counts as a burden?* Bags did — that was settled. But Juno's fire had an upkeep, a continuous feed of attention that kept a called flame alive, and upkeep was a cost, and the text said *what you carry costs them nothing*.

"Rules," Doc said, setting a kitchen timer on the mat between them. "Juno lights a working flame and holds it. Ren takes the feed if it'll go. First symptom — the *first* one, child, not the one you think you can push through — you say so, and we stop. This is a measurement, not a corridor run."

Juno called fire to her hands, a working flame the size of a loaf of bread, and shaped it into a slow ring. Ren reached for it the way he'd reached for her duffel — *take up her burden* — and the feed came across.

It was a firehose.

He'd expected weight. It wasn't weight; it was spending, continuous and huge, attention pouring out of him into the ring at a rate that made no sense for something the size of bread. There was no efficiency in him to soften it — the text moved the cost, not the skill of paying it, and Juno's costs were being billed to a man with no fire in him at all. He held it for one minute. He held it for two. The ring burned on and Juno stood there with her hands open and empty of effort, and her face did something complicated, because for the first time in her career she was casting for free.

At three minutes the edges of the room started their slide toward tunnel, and Ren said, "Symptom," out loud like a good student, and Doc said "Done," and the feed snapped back to Juno, who closed her fist and put the ring out.

Ren sat on the mat and did the arithmetic he'd just paid for. Three minutes of one small ring, held steady, no shaping, no throwing. It had cost him most of a morning's envelope.

"Juno." He waited until she looked at him. "That rate. That's your *baseline*? That's a candle for you?"

"It's — okay, so, everyone's feed reads different, you can't really compare—"

"You spend like that every session. Every Gate. And then you shape it, and throw it, and hold three of them when you're scared." He was talking too plainly, and he knew it, and he didn't stop. "That's why you're empty by the back third. It's not discipline. You're paying wholesale for everything and you've never once seen anyone else's bill."

The gym was quiet. Juno looked at her hands.

"My old team ran the numbers once," she said. "The trainer said I was uneconomical." She said the word carefully, like it was someone else's. "They drilled me on ration schedules for a year. It didn't take."

"Ration schedules assume you don't want to spend." Ren thought of his mother, working a third shift so that Bea wouldn't have to know the word *arrears*. "You want to spend. Every mark, every time, so nothing gets through. That's not uneconomical. It's just expensive."

Juno laughed, wobbly. "Okay. New team rule. Nobody audits me except the butler." She dug in her bag and put a container of leftover noodles in front of him with great firmness. "Eat. You look like the grey."

He ate. Behind him he heard Doc say to Cass, quietly, in the tone of a man closing a file: "Break-glass only. In a Gate, that trick is for the day nothing else is left."

Cass wrote it on the whiteboard, in the corner reserved for rules, under *nobody goes in unread*.

---

Saturday night, day eight, the plan filled the whole whiteboard.

Pip had built two maps of the Stacks, because the survey was two years stale and she refused to pretend otherwise. The dry map trusted the Year 20 charts. The wet map assumed the flood had climbed since — deeper water in the low thirds, the sluice choke half-drowned, the racking canyons undercut. Two routes, one decision point.

"First junction," she said, tapping it. "The survey says the water mark is on the second pillar. If it's higher, we're on the wet map and we go up and over the racking early. If somebody sees water where the chart says floor, we don't discuss it, we just switch."

"And if it's worse than the wet map?" Theo asked.

"Then the plan is Cass's plan."

"The plan," Cass said, "is we leave. A qualifier scores on the season. It doesn't score on your funeral." She turned to Ren. "Your spending plan. Say it back."

"Escort at the three named hazards — the racking traverse, the sluice choke, the Core approach. Carry is Doc's pack and nothing else. No upkeep tricks. First symptom, I call it, and after that I'm a man holding a bag." It had taken two days of drills for that last part to stop stinging. "Grey-out inside a Gate means someone falls. I'm not going grey inside a Gate."

"Good." Cass looked around the room, at each of them, one at a time, the way she'd read his printout — twice, top to bottom. "We've never cleared a C. Here's what I know that the table doesn't. Nobody in this division has ever taken a support build into a Gate. Every wipe in the Stacks was a team that had to choose between speed and footing. We don't have to choose. We are going to walk in slow, and read everything, and stumble exactly zero times, and the broadcast is going to be the most boring clear of the weekend." She capped the marker. "Sleep. Van's at seven."

---

The house smelled like garlic when Ren got home, which was wrong, because his mother worked Saturday nights.

She was at the stove. She'd swapped the shift — he found out later it had cost her a favour she'd been saving for two years — and she didn't say anything about Gates or broadcasts or the fact that the last time their family had stood near an aperture, it had been coming the other way through District Nine. She fed him until he pushed the plate away, and then she put one more spoonful on it anyway.

"Your sister showed me the spreadsheet," Ada said, sitting down across from him. "The black row."

"It's just the arrears. The rest is still—"

"I know what it is." She was quiet for a moment. On the fridge, under the lemon magnet, the stamped receipt sat on top of the letter it answered. "Nineteen years I've been telling you two: read everything twice, sign nothing you don't understand. I meant forms." She looked at him, and she wasn't crying, which he could tell was a decision being actively made. "Tomorrow, whatever's in that place — you read it twice. You hear me? Twice."

"Yes, mum."

"Then that's all I have to say about it." It wasn't, and they both sat there while she didn't say the rest.

Bea appeared long enough to steal the last spring roll and put her phone face-up on the table: the League app, the fixture page. GRAVE SOCIETY — THE STACKS (C) — SUNDAY 09:00. She'd set an alert for it. Below the fixture, the prediction bar sat where the whole world had left it: seventy-first of seventy-one.

"wrong," she said, to the phone, and went back to her room.

Ren washed up, checked his kit by the door — spare everything, because he'd learned that from Juno — and lay in bed doing what he always did with numbers. The trouble was that Gates didn't publish their numbers. Four wipes since Year 19. Eleven minutes, median. Two maps, one junction, three hazards, one hard stop. He turned the figures over for an hour and they refused to add up to anything, because the number that mattered wasn't on any chart Pip could draw — it was six people, tomorrow at nine, on every screen in Halden.

He'd read his own skills two hundred times. Tomorrow the Stacks got to read them back.

He set two alarms and, eventually, slept.
